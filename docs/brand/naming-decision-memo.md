## Purpose and how to read this memo

This memo consolidates the naming work for the new subsidiary competing with AccuLynx: 18 candidates across four buckets (Vantrow-play, AccuLynx-play, hybrid, standalone), each run through a first-pass automated screen for domain availability, name collisions, and US trademark exposure on 2026-07-15. Every candidate gets a summary row, a detail section, and a risk grade (low / medium / elevated). The screens are automated quick-passes — they are inputs to a decision, not legal clearance, and nothing here substitutes for a professional trademark search.

One point stated plainly up front: the **AccuLynx-evocation bucket carries structurally elevated trademark risk**. AccuLynx is a well-funded incumbent operating in the same trademark classes (9/42), selling to the same customers, and any name built to evoke theirs — shared "Accu-" prefix, "-lynx"-style suffix, or the same precision-prefix-plus-predator formula — invites a likelihood-of-confusion fight we would probably lose, or at minimum pay heavily to litigate. That bucket is included because it was requested, but **the winner should probably come from another bucket unless counsel affirmatively clears one of them**.

---

## Brand-architecture recommendation: endorsed brand

**Recommendation: a standalone name with the endorsement line "a Vantrow company," on its own domain.**

Reasoning: an endorsed brand protects the Vantrow parent — if the roofing product stumbles, gets sued over its name, or has to be renamed, the damage is contained in the subsidiary brand rather than splashing onto Vantrow itself. It also gives the product its own dashboard story: contractors log into a product with its own name, logo, and domain, while the "a Vantrow company" line quietly supplies credibility, financial backing, and a family narrative for press and enterprise buyers. Finally, the pattern is reusable: subsidiary #2 (siding, gutters, solar, or whatever comes next) simply becomes "[Name], a Vantrow company" — the architecture scales without any renaming or umbrella-brand gymnastics. Several shortlisted names (notably Kestrow and Trowen) carry a phonetic echo of the parent, which makes the endorsement line read as natural family rather than bolt-on.
