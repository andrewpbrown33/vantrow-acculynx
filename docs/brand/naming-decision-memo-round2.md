## How to read this memo

Every candidate has (1) a row in the summary table, (2) a detail section with rationale,
tagline, confirmed domain status + evidence, collision findings, trademark notes, and a
risk grade with its reason. Risk is graded **low / medium / elevated** on the same rubric
as Round 1:

- **low** — clean domains, no in-industry entity on the name, no live in-class mark, no
  confusing similarity to AccuLynx. A soft "watch-item" (e.g. a descriptive root) may
  remain but nobody actually uses the name.
- **medium** — clean of any hard collision, but held back by a crowded near-namespace
  (a descriptive industry root others use) and/or a minor collision in an unrelated field.
- **elevated** — an exact-name entity exists in construction, or a near-identical software
  brand sits in an adjacent/overlapping vertical. Usable only if counsel affirmatively
  clears it.

> ### These are first-pass automated screens, NOT legal clearance
>
> Domains were checked via registry RDAP (Verisign for `.com`; Identity Digital / rdap.org
> for `.io`) cross-checked against Google DNS-over-HTTPS. Collisions were checked by web
> search. **Trademark checks could not query USPTO tmsearch directly** — it is a JS-only
> single-page app that renders no data to a fetcher, its API rejects POSTs (405), and
> aggregator mirrors frequently return 403 — so every trademark finding here rests on
> web-search indexing, a non-authoritative method that misses recent, pending, state, and
> common-law filings. A professional clearance search per
> `docs/runbooks/05-legal-counsel-checklist.md` is required before adopting any name.
>
> Known `.io` caveat carried over from Round 1: rdap.org returns 404 for some *registered*
> `.io` domains (bootstrap gap). Every `.io` verdict below was cross-checked against DNS NS
> records and, where reachable, the authoritative Identity Digital registry RDAP.

## Top 5 recommendation

This round produced more genuinely clean options than Round 1, so five are surfaced rather
than a single shortlist. Ranked best-first.

### 1. Eaverow — *why it wins:* the only candidate that is both **low-risk** and carries a
built-in Vantrow echo. Both `.com` and `.io` are confirmed available, there is **no
roofing/construction/software entity on the name**, AccuLynx similarity is **none**, and the
suggestive "eave" root is a brand asset (registrable, not descriptive) for a roofing product.
The `-row` tail makes "Eaverow, a Vantrow company" read as natural family.
**Biggest weakness:** phonetic proximity to Eavor, a notable geothermal-energy firm (EE-vor vs
EE-ver-oh) — unrelated industry, so a footnote, but worth a verbal-handoff / cross-class
citation check with counsel.

### 2. Gablora — *why it wins:* the other **low-risk** option — zero collisions across five
targeted searches, both TLDs clean, no trademark surfaced, and a warm, expansive coinage with
clear headroom across exteriors (not roof-locked). Pronounceable and distinctive as a whole
invented word. **Biggest weakness:** it embeds "gable," a generic roofing term, placing it near
a crowded field of gable-named roofers and slightly softening in-class distinctiveness; and
AccuLynx similarity is graded "low" rather than "none." No `-row`/parent echo.

### 3. Battenly — *why it wins:* zero AccuLynx similarity, no name collision of any kind, both
TLDs available, and an insider-credible root ("batten," plus the "batten down" weatherproofing
nod) with a friendly, brandable "-ly" finish. **Biggest weakness:** "batten" is descriptive and
the Batten-named roofing field is moderately crowded (physical contractors, not software),
which caps distinctiveness and lands it at medium; `.io` rests on DNS evidence only.

### 4. Hewdeck — *why it wins:* short, punchy, two syllables, zero AccuLynx similarity, and no
in-industry collision — an evocative Hew+deck compound grounded in the building envelope.
**Biggest weakness:** it sits inside a "-deck" *software* cluster with Hookdeck (an established
webhook-infra SaaS) as a phonetic/visual near-neighbor, and "deck" is a descriptive roofing
term; `.io` rests on DNS evidence only (RDAP inconclusive).

### 5. Trowforge — *why it wins:* carries the clearest parent echo of the five (the "trow" of
Van-trow), zero AccuLynx similarity, both TLDs available, and a strong craft/"forge" story with
expansion headroom across all exterior trades. **Biggest weakness:** the identical two-word
string "Trow Forge" is used by an unrelated packaging firm (a prior common-law user), and
"-forge" is heavily diluted among roofing/construction companies — both medium-grade drags on
distinctiveness.

### #1 pick: **Eaverow**

Eaverow is the recommendation. It is the only option that clears every gate at once — a
confirmed-available `.com` (plus `.io`), the lowest risk grade in the field, zero confusing
similarity to AccuLynx, and a suggestive roofing-native root ("eave") that is a genuine
trademark asset rather than a descriptive liability, with obvious headroom from the roof edge
outward across exteriors. It is easy to say and spell after one hearing, and the `-row` tail
lets "Eaverow, a Vantrow company" land as a real family name rather than a bolt-on endorsement.
Its one caveat — phonetic proximity to the geothermal firm Eavor — is unrelated-industry and
belongs on counsel's checklist, not in the way of registering the domain today.
